Q&A with

Kate Doheny
Creative Director
& Principal

as told to Will Speros,
a Brooklyn-based design journalist

Tell me about yourself.

I'm a designer, and the founder and principal of Doheny Atelier. Since 2013, my freelance practice has grown into a multidisciplinary agency specializing in creative and design direction. With expertise in packaging design, my agency offers creative strategy and branding services.

How does your work stand out?

I'm not afraid to take risks. I'm a huge believer in going beyond the brief. Sometimes clients want to go safe, other times they want to go way out there. Very often, they don't know which they want until they actually see it. I'm here to show them.

What types of clients do you look for?

I treat each project with the same meticulousness, whether the client is a startup or a Fortune 500 company. New clients mean new ways of approaching a challenge, and I like working with all different types of clients to maintain my versatility as a designer. Sometimes I'm working with the CEO, sometimes I'm working with the creator, sometimes I'm working with the marketing team. I really appreciate people who are direct with their feedback. Even if they don't know what they want yet, we'll work on that together and get where they're trying to go.

"With this job, there's almost an endless amount of ideas, so I love that I keep uncovering something new."

How do you ensure quality?

I ensure options are refined and limited before client exposure. On the other hand, sometimes it makes more sense to work closely with the client throughout the process, too—showing untidy work, rough drafts, and building from there.

I'm also a problem solver. I'm very solution-oriented in my design. It's not just function and it's not just beauty. Sometimes I may want to lean on the beauty of it and figure out the function later, and sometimes it's the opposite. But to ensure quality, you need both.

What do your formative projects communicate about you as a designer?

Kylie Cosmetics was evolving and needed greater cohesion. Capturing the founder's own creative growth was essential as well, and in my collaboration with her, I built a design language to elevate the brand beyond a young buzzy image. This established clearer connectivity across collections and reflected the founder's many creative inspirations. I had to remain open to a more organic workflow, releasing my creativity in a new way.

With a brand like Obagi, I was ushering a company from the 1980s into the contemporary market. I combined their five or six different logos into a cohesive touchpoint that encapsulated the overall brand. I then created a hierarchical packaging system for the different sub-brands that was easy for consumers to understand. It was a really fulfilling challenge to stretch my design muscles and empower this beloved brand to attract modern consumers while honoring longstanding customers.

What's your favorite part of your work?

I'm so exhilarated every day because I can't believe I'm able to do this for my career. I really do love having a creative solution to a problem. I love feeling like I've found the answer no one has thought of before. With this job, there's almost an endless amount of ideas, so I love that I keep uncovering something new.
